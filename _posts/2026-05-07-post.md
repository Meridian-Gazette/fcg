# The Surgeon's Hands

He had the steadiest hands in the hospital. Thirty years, four thousand surgeries, not a single tremor. Colleagues called him "The Machine." He never corrected them. He liked it.

When the Parkinson's diagnosis came, he told no one. He simply began operating faster, racing against the calendar. He completed two hundred more surgeries before anyone noticed the faintest shake in his left pinky.

They gave him a retirement dinner. They gave him a plaque. They gave him a watch, which he found almost cruel.

At home, alone, he tried to thread a needle. Then he tried again. Then again. He sat at his kitchen table until morning, surrounded by thread, unable to stop trying.

It was the first time in his life he understood his patients—people trapped inside bodies that refused to obey them. He realized, with a horror that felt like falling, that he had never once truly understood what he was saving them *from*.

He had only ever understood the cutting.
